F.E.A.S.T.

Technology Infrastructure for Caregiver Support

Director of Technology Services · 2020–Present

F.E.A.S.T. supports families navigating eating disorder caregiving and treatment, reaching 12,000+ families each month through its online programs and services. As the only technical staff, I build and maintain the systems that connect families to evidence-based resources and peer support—and in this work, the way we structure information shapes what actions feel possible for caregivers in crisis. This includes CRM infrastructure, timed content delivery systems, analytics tools, and the organization's AI policy. As a donation-based nonprofit, improvements are incremental but steady, with ongoing UX testing (heatmaps, usability research) to better serve parents and caregivers in difficult circumstances.

FEAST 30 Days: Computational Scaffolding Before the Age of AI

Automated curriculum delivery system · 17,000+ families served

F.E.A.S.T.'s clinical team designed a 30-day curriculum that scaffolds caregivers through early crisis, sequencing information based on what parents can absorb at each stage. My role was building and maintaining the delivery infrastructure—first in Mailchimp, then rebuilt in Salesforce with parallel static web/PDF versions. I made the technical decisions: timing logic, segmentation rules, content triggers, engagement tracking. Building this system gave me direct insight into how timed information delivery shapes user experience during crisis. It's computational scaffolding that predates the AI systems I want to study.

The 30-Day Arc

Days 1–3
Reframing the crisis

From blame to understanding

Days 4–7
Practical foundations

Diagnosis, nutrition, mealtime strategies

Days 8–10
Building external supports

Treatment options, identifying assets

Days 11–14
Family and emotional work

Family dynamics, siblings, distress tolerance

Days 15–20
Harder truths once stabilized

Medical consequences, weight, body image

Days 21–23
The scary stuff, delayed until ready

Extreme behaviors, self-harm risk

Days 24–28
The longer view

Self-care, recovery trajectories

Days 29–30
Transition out of crisis mode

Relapse prevention, moving forward

Design Logic

The sequencing isn't random. Day 1 doesn't hit parents with "self-harm and suicidality"—that's Day 23, after they've built capacity. The system shapes what actions feel possible at each stage.
