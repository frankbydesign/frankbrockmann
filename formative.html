Background

During my recovery from a hit-and-run injury in 2024, I used AI tools extensively: over a thousand conversations across more than six months. Somewhere in that process, a question emerged that I couldn't shake: was this helping me reconnect with people, or was it replacing the relationships I actually needed?

The literature on AI scaffolding focuses on students and curricula. But millions of adults are using AI to figure out their lives, not just their homework.

These two papers are my attempt to frame the question:

Formative AI Scaffolding proposes a criterion for distinguishing AI use that prepares people for community from AI use that substitutes for it.

Beyond Capabilities examines the same tension from an organizational angle: how mission-driven groups might adopt AI without losing what matters.

The Question That Emerged

The distinction I keep coming back to is community direction. AI scaffolding feels formative when it prepares you for membership in human communities—of practice, relationship, or purpose. It starts to feel habit-forming when the loop closes on itself: more conversation, more refinement, but no movement toward the people and places where development actually happens.

I don't think this means all AI use should be instrumental, or that solitude has no value. But when adults use AI to navigate major life decisions, something shifts depending on where the conversation points. The person who uses AI to research a career change, rehearse difficult conversations, and build confidence to approach potential mentors is being scaffolded toward community. The person who finds AI conversation sufficient in itself—who stops seeking human connection because AI meets their immediate needs—is in a different situation, even if both report satisfaction.

I went looking for research that addressed this and mostly found educational scaffolding literature—Vygotsky, Wood et al.—which assumes predetermined curricula, external assessors, and bounded timeframes. Adults using AI for life decisions have none of these. The questions are existential rather than curricular: not "how do I learn this skill?" but "what kind of life should I build?"

The second paper, Beyond Capabilities, came out of watching this same tension play out in organizations. When mission-driven groups adopt AI based on what it can do rather than what they should do, they risk drifting from their core purpose. KĀʻEO and F.E.A.S.T. became my design cases for thinking through how that drift might be prevented.

I don't know if "community direction" is exactly the right criterion. These papers are starting points—hypothesis-generating rather than conclusive. But the research directions that follow from them are what I hope to pursue.
