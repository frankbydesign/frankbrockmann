Computational Infrastructure for Crisis Care

F.E.A.S.T. supports 15,000+ families annually navigating eating disorder care and treatment—a population in crisis which is often making high-stakes decisions with limited information.

Since 2020, I've worked inside this organization's computational infrastructure: shaping how automated messages reach families in distress, establishing what AI should and shouldn't do in a care context, and building tools that surface patterns leadership couldn't see.

What happens when "helping someone" becomes a database operation?

Language & Communication

Voice & Tone Guide

When families contact F.E.A.S.T. in crisis, they encounter communications across multiple channels—email sequences, social media, website copy, support group facilitation. These touchpoints are often templated, scheduled, or produced at scale. I developed a voice and tone guide that establishes guardrails for how the organization speaks to people in distress. The constraints are specific: empathetic but not effusive, knowledgeable but not clinical, encouraging without false promises. Short sentences. Limited exclamation marks. Comfort and reassurance in every message. This isn't a style guide about fonts and colors. It's a set of design decisions about what care should sound like when it's mediated by templates, automation, and organizational scale.

Research question: How do you systematize empathy? What gets preserved—and what gets lost—when compassionate communication must be reproducible across staff, volunteers, and channels?

Governance & Ethics

AI Governance Policy

View Full Policy

As AI tools became accessible to small nonprofits, F.E.A.S.T. faced decisions most organizations were ignoring: Where should AI be used? Where should it be prohibited? Who decides? I drafted the organization's AI policy, now published on their website. The core position: AI may improve internal operations, but it will never mediate direct support. No chatbots. No automated crisis response. No situation where a caregiver might believe they're talking to a human when they're not. The policy also addresses data protection (no training on F.E.A.S.T. inputs), requires human oversight on all AI outputs, and names risks specific to this population: misinformation about eating disorders, bias in how bodies are discussed, tone failures in sensitive contexts, and erosion of trust in human connection.

Research question: How do small organizations establish AI governance that protects vulnerable populations? What frameworks help non-technical leaders make these decisions?

Decision Support

Donor Pattern Analyzer

View Live Tool

Six years of donation data. 3,400+ donors. Patterns invisible in spreadsheets. I built an interactive analytics tool that surfaces concentration risk, retention trends, lapse predictions, and data quality flags—designed to scaffold executive decision-making about organizational sustainability. The tool contextualizes performance against sector benchmarks and economic conditions, helping leadership distinguish "is this us?" from "is this everyone?" This wasn't a consulting deliverable. It emerged from years of watching how leadership struggled to see patterns in their own data—and asking what kind of computational media might augment that capacity.

Research question: How do different visual representations of risk change nonprofit leaders' prioritization decisions? What does it mean to design for augmented judgment rather than automated answers?

These three artifacts sit at different layers of the same organization—language, governance, decision-making. But they share a common thread: each required designing constraints that shape how humans experience care, or how leaders make decisions about sustaining care.

A tone guide determines what support sounds like at scale. An AI policy draws boundaries around where automation belongs. A visualization tool surfaces what spreadsheets hide. None of these are neutral technical choices. Each embeds assumptions about what matters, what's visible, and who has agency. That's the terrain I want to investigate as a researcher: the computational mediation of care practices, and the design decisions that make those systems more—or less—humane.
