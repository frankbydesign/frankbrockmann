F.E.A.S.T.

Technology Infrastructure for Caregiver Support

Director of Technology Services · 2020–Present

F.E.A.S.T. supports families navigating eating disorder caregiving and treatment, reaching 12,000+ families each month through its online programs and services. As the only technical staff, I build and maintain the systems that connect families to evidence-based resources and peer support—and in this work, the way we structure information shapes what actions feel possible for caregivers in crisis.

As a donation-based nonprofit, improvements are incremental but steady, with ongoing UX testing (heatmaps, usability research) to better serve parents and caregivers in difficult circumstances.

My Role

I serve as the director of technology services, responsible for all systems architecture, implementation, and maintenance; this includes everything from WordPress and Salesforce work to Google Admin duties, podcast production, and whatever else the community needs next. I directed production of the FEAST 30 Days delivery system (see below), and developed numerous products from scratch: the AI ethics policy, the donor pattern analyzer, the two-page flyer, and the voice/tone guidelines (see right).

The ongoing challenge: designing for people in crisis who don't have bandwidth for complicated interfaces. This work shapes how I think about technology in service of community.

Collaborators

Judy Krasna (Executive Director) provides organizational leadership and strategy. She and the volunteers developed the 30-day curriculum content; my role was designing and building the delivery system.

FEAST 30 Days: Computational Scaffolding Before the Age of AI

Automated curriculum delivery system · 17,000+ families served

F.E.A.S.T.'s clinical team designed a 30-day curriculum that scaffolds caregivers through early crisis, sequencing information based on what parents can absorb at each stage. My role was building and maintaining the delivery infrastructure (first in Mailchimp, then rebuilt in Salesforce with parallel static web/PDF versions). I made the technical decisions: timing, flow logic, segmentation, engagement tracking for both English and Spanish versions of the program.

Building this system gave me direct insight into how timed information delivery shapes the user experience for community members in crisis. It's computational scaffolding that predates the AI systems I want to study.

The 30-Day Arc

Days 1–3
Reframing the crisis

From blame to understanding

Days 4–7
Practical foundations

Diagnosis, nutrition, mealtime strategies

Days 8–10
Building external supports

Treatment options, identifying assets

Days 11–14
Family and emotional work

Family dynamics, siblings, distress tolerance

Days 15–20
Harder truths once stabilized

Medical consequences, weight, body image

Days 21–23
The scary stuff, delayed until ready

Extreme behaviors, self-harm risk

Days 24–28
The longer view

Self-care, recovery trajectories

Days 29–30
Transition out of crisis mode

Relapse prevention, moving forward

Design Logic

The sequencing isn't random. For example, Day 1 doesn't hit parents with "self-harm and suicidality" because that needs to wait until Day 23, after they've built capacity. The system helps to shape what actions feel possible at each stage.
