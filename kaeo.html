Portfolio KĀʻEO Project
KĀʻEO · Assessment Consultant & AI Lab Co-Designer · 2016–present

AI Tools and Computational Systems Infrastructure to Support Hawaiian Language Assessment and Revitalization

KĀʻEO is the only Hawaiian-medium assessment system in the world—and the only native language assessment used for federal accountability in the United States. Since 2016, I've worked with the program as an assessment consultant. In 2024, I co-designed an AI workflow that solved a problem nobody else had cracked.

Psychometricians analyzing test performance don't speak Hawaiian. Hawaiian language educators aren't trained in psychometrics. When a test item performs poorly, figuring out why requires both perspectives—but they couldn't easily work together. Geography, language barriers, and the need to protect Hawaiian content created bottlenecks that slowed everything down.

Hawaiian language data has been extracted and exploited before. We designed the system so nothing leaves the room without cultural review. AI as augmentation, not automation—humans are the loop, not just in it.

AI Workflow Design

AI-Assisted Item Analysis Lab

We built a secure environment where language models help bridge the gap between statistical analysis and cultural expertise. NotebookLM processes psychometric reports and flags patterns across dozens of items. Claude translates technical findings into plain-language briefs. Hawaiian educators review everything before it goes anywhere. Nothing leaves without cultural sign-off. The key insight: AI works here not as replacement but as translator and aggregator, always under human oversight.

Research question: How do you design AI workflows that augment expert judgment without extracting or exposing protected cultural content?

Assessment Translation

Interpretive Brief Framework

In October 2024, we analyzed 58 flagged items across Hawaiian Language Arts, Math, and Science. Six interpretive briefs identified real issues: linguistic ambiguity, depth-of-knowledge misalignment, structural overload. The briefs translate psychometric signals into actionable feedback for content developers—without requiring them to understand factor analysis or DIF statistics. Each brief follows a structured format: statistical flag, plain-language interpretation, hypothesized cause, and recommended action.

Research question: What is lost—or gained—when technical knowledge is translated across expertise boundaries? How do translation artifacts shape downstream decisions?

Governance & Sovereignty

Cultural Review Protocol

The workflow embeds explicit checkpoints where Hawaiian language experts review AI-generated outputs before they leave the secure environment. This isn't just quality assurance—it's sovereignty protection. Hawaiian content has historically been extracted, mistranslated, and misappropriated. The protocol ensures that computational tools serve the community's goals without creating new extraction risks. Every output is reviewed for cultural accuracy, appropriate framing, and alignment with ʻŌlelo Hawaiʻi revitalization values.

Research question: How do indigenous communities maintain authority over their own data when AI systems are involved? What governance structures preserve sovereignty while enabling beneficial applications?

This work sits at the intersection of educational measurement, AI ethics, and indigenous data sovereignty. The technical challenge—bridging psychometric and linguistic expertise—led to deeper questions about how computational systems can serve communities without extracting from them.

The KĀʻEO project demonstrates that AI tools can work in culturally sensitive contexts when designed with community authority at the center. That's a pattern I want to investigate further: the conditions under which computational mediation supports—rather than undermines—human expertise and cultural self-determination.
