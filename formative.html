Background

I used AI extensively during my recovery from a hit-and-run injury—over a thousand conversations across months of recovery time. Somewhere in that process, a question emerged that I couldn't shake: was this helping me reconnect with people, or was it replacing the relationships I actually needed?

I looked for research that addressed this and mostly didn't find it. The literature on AI scaffolding focuses on students and curricula. But millions of adults are using AI to figure out their lives, not just their homework.

These papers are my attempt to start framing the question:

The primary Formative AI Scaffolding paper proposes a criterion for distinguishing AI use that prepares people for community from AI use that substitutes for it.

The Beyond Capabilities paper, based on my direct experience with two organizations, examines the same tension from an organizational angle: how mission-driven groups might adopt AI without losing what matters.

I don't know if the "community criterion" is the right frame. These are starting points, not conclusions. But I think the question is real and worth investigating—and it connects to what I've seen in my applied work at KĀʻEO and F.E.A.S.T., where design choices about how information is structured shape what actions feel possible for people in transition.
